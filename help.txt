The simplest way to recover a dropped column in Pandas depends on whether you have a copy or need to reload the source data. [1, 2] 
## 1. Re-run your "Load" Cell
If you loaded your data from a CSV or database at the start of your notebook, the easiest fix is simply to re-run the cell where you first defined the dataframe. This resets the df variable to its original state with all columns included. [1] 
## 2. Check for a Backup
If you created a copy of the dataframe earlier in your code (e.g., df_copy = df.copy()), you can restore the missing column from that copy: [3] 

# Copy the missing column from your backup back to the main df
df['missing_column'] = df_copy['missing_column']

## 3. Re-run your Processing Steps
If you cannot reload the entire dataset, you can often just restart your kernel and run all cells (usually a button at the top of your editor like Jupyter or VS Code). This ensures all your cleaning steps happen again, but you should delete or comment out the line that dropped the column first:

# df.drop(columns=['column_name'], inplace=True)  <-- Comment this out!

## 4. Merging (If you have two DataFrames)
If you have one dataframe with the column and your "current" one without it, you can merge them back together using their index:

# Re-attach 'missing_column' from the source to your current dfdf = df.merge(original_df[['missing_column']], left_index=True, right_index=True)

Pro Tip: In the future, avoid using inplace=True when dropping columns. Instead, assign the result to a new variable (e.g., df_cleaned = df.drop(...)). This keeps your original df safe and intact! [4, 5]
